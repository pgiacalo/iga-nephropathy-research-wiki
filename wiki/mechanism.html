Disease Mechanism — The 4-Hit Model

Each hit is a distinct, druggable node. Prevention aims at the early hits; reversal aims at the late ones.

Last updated: 2026-07-18 · First discussed in Conversation 1

IgA nephropathy is driven by a well-characterized four-step cascade. The value of the model is that every hit is a separate therapeutic target, which lets us map candidate treatments cleanly onto our two research aims.

Hit 1 — Overproduction of galactose-deficient IgA1 (Gd-IgA1)

Mucosal B cells produce IgA1 whose hinge-region O-glycans are under-galactosylated, exposing N-acetylgalactosamine (GalNAc). This is the malformed protein at the center of the disease. Contributing factors:

  • Aberrant mucosal immunity — gut-associated lymphoid tissue (Peyer's patches) and the APRIL / BAFF cytokine axis drive excess IgA1 production.
  • Glycosyltransferase dysregulation — reduced activity of C1GALT1 and its chaperone Cosmc, the enzymes that add galactose to the hinge glycans.
Why it matters for us: Hit 1 is where the "fix the malformed protein / prevent it from malforming" aim lives. See Aim 1.

Hit 2 — Anti-glycan autoantibodies

Circulating IgG (and some IgA) autoantibodies recognize the exposed GalNAc epitope on Gd-IgA1. Their production is what turns a glycosylation quirk into an autoimmune process.

Hit 3 — Immune complex formation

Gd-IgA1 and the anti-glycan autoantibodies bind to form circulating immune complexes. These are larger, clearance-resistant, and pathogenic.

Hit 4 — Mesangial deposition, complement activation, injury

Immune complexes deposit in the glomerular mesangium ("clogging" the filter), then activate complement — predominantly the alternative and lectin pathways (the classical pathway is largely spared). This drives mesangial proliferation, inflammation, proteinuria, and ultimately glomerulosclerosis and interstitial fibrosis.

The hard problem: once fibrosis is established, reversing it is the least-solved part of the field. See Aim 2.
