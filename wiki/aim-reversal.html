Aim 2 · Reverse Kidney Damage

Halt and, ideally, reverse the injury at the deposition/complement/fibrosis stage (Hit 4).

Last updated: 2026-07-18 · Conv 1, statuses verified in Conv 2

This aim targets the downstream hit: the immune complexes have already deposited in the mesangium, so the goal is to shut down the injury cascade and repair what has been damaged.

Complement inhibition

IgAN activates the alternative and lectin complement pathways. Blocking them reduces glomerular inflammation.

  • Iptacopan (Fabhalta) (Factor B inhibitor, oral) — full FDA approval Jul 2026 (accelerated 2024); alternative pathway.
  • Ravulizumab (Ultomiris) (long-acting anti-C5 mAb) — Phase 3 (I CAN).
  • Pegcetacoplan (C3 inhibitor) — Phase 2 for IgAN (approved for the different indications C3G / IC-MPGN).
  • Cemdisiran (C5 siRNA) — Phase 2.
  • Narsoplimab (anti-MASP-2, lectin pathway) — discontinued; the Phase 3 ARTEMIS-IGAN trial failed its proteinuria endpoint (2023) and the program was halted for IgAN.

Hemodynamic / anti-fibrotic (RAAS & endothelin)

  • Sparsentan (Filspari) (dual endothelin-A + angiotensin-II receptor antagonist) — full FDA approval Sep 2024; reduces proteinuria and slows progression (PROTECT).
  • Atrasentan (Vanrafia) (selective endothelin-A antagonist) — FDA accelerated approval Apr 2025.
  • SGLT2 inhibitors (e.g., dapagliflozin) — slow fibrosis / eGFR decline; KDIGO foundational therapy.
  • Finerenone (non-steroidal mineralocorticoid receptor antagonist) — anti-inflammatory / anti-fibrotic; approved in CKD/T2D, being studied with SGLT2i.
  • RAAS blockade (ACE inhibitors / ARBs) — long-standing background standard of care.
The unsolved core of this aim: the therapies above mostly slow or halt progression — no approved IgAN therapy is proven to reverse established sclerosis. But reversal is not impossible: human proof-of-concept exists (diabetic nephropathy lesions regressed 10 years after pancreas transplant once the driver was removed), and podocyte/progenitor repair can drive glomerular remission. The realistic near-term win is maximizing regression in the reversible window — remove the driver early, before scar matures. Full deep-dive: Reverse Established Fibrosis.

Open questions for this aim

  • Which complement pathway (alternative vs. lectin) dominates injury, and does it differ by patient?
  • Is early fibrosis reversible, and what cellular signals (e.g., myofibroblast de-differentiation) would drive reversal?
  • Can anti-fibrotic or regenerative approaches be combined with upstream Aim 1 therapies for additive benefit?
