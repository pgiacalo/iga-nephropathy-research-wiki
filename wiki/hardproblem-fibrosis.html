Hard Problem · Reverse Established Kidney Fibrosis

The frontier of Aim 2 — moving from halting progression to actually regenerating damaged nephrons.

Last updated: 2026-07-18 · Promoted to its own page in Conversation 2

Why this page exists: current IgAN therapy (complement inhibitors, sparsentan, SGLT2i, RAAS blockade) largely slows or halts decline. True reversal of established glomerulosclerosis and tubulointerstitial fibrosis — restoring lost function — is the highest-value, least-solved goal. Sources are listed at the bottom of this page and in References.

Is kidney fibrosis reversible at all?

Partial reversibility is plausible and supported in animal models and some human settings: relieving the driving injury can allow regression of early/immature fibrosis and functional recovery. However, advanced, cross-linked scar with nephron loss is effectively irreversible — nephrons do not regenerate in adult human kidney. The practical target is therefore the reversible window: active, cellular, early fibrosis before architecture is destroyed.

Cellular drivers (the levers to pull)

  • Myofibroblast activation is the central effector — matrix-producing cells arising largely from resident fibroblasts/pericytes. Getting them to de-activate / de-differentiate or undergo apoptosis is the key to regression.
  • TGF-β signaling is the dominant pro-fibrotic pathway (with CTGF, Wnt/β-catenin, Hedgehog as amplifiers). Broad TGF-β blockade has been limited by on-target toxicity — specificity is the challenge.
  • Tubular epithelial injury & cell-cycle arrest (a "senescent," pro-fibrotic secretory state) sustains the fibrotic signal; the classic "tubular EMT" idea is now viewed as mostly paracrine signaling rather than epithelial cells literally becoming fibroblasts.
  • Chronic inflammation & hypoxia (rarefaction of peritubular capillaries) lock in the scar.

What actually halts vs. reverses today

InterventionEffect on damage
RAAS blockade (ACEi/ARB), sparsentanSlows; reduces proteinuria-driven injury
SGLT2 inhibitorsSlows eGFR decline; anti-fibrotic/metabolic; strong renoprotection
Complement inhibitors, APRIL/BAFF, NefeconRemove the upstream driver → may permit regression of active lesions
Dedicated anti-fibrotic / regenerative agentsNot yet established for kidney

Emerging / experimental directions

  • Targeted anti-fibrotics — pathway-selective TGF-β/CTGF modulation, anti-senescence ("senolytic") strategies to clear pro-fibrotic tubular cells.
  • Regenerative / cell approaches — mesenchymal-stromal-cell and secretome strategies aiming to shift the milieu from scarring to repair (largely preclinical for kidney).
  • Combination logic — pair an upstream Aim 1 driver-removal with a pro-regenerative agent, so that stopping new injury is coupled to healing existing injury.
Strategic takeaway: the realistic near-term win is maximizing regression in the reversible window — aggressively remove the driver early (Aims 1 & upstream Aim 2) so cellular fibrosis resolves before it matures. Genuine regeneration of lost nephrons remains a long-horizon research goal.

Linked open questions

  • How do we identify the reversible window in a patient (biomarkers/biopsy features of "active" vs. "fixed" fibrosis)?
  • Can myofibroblast de-differentiation be induced safely in kidney?
  • Do driver-removal + regenerative combinations outperform either alone?
